Bales, Mary Ann

Birth Name Bales, Mary Ann

Narrative

Mary Ann, always known as Polly, was born in 1868. She was a delightful woman, always immaculate in her person, neat and smart, gay and friendly. She married Fred Smith, a dapper, fussy man who sold material by the yard in a draper's shop. Their only child, Reina, was most attactive in appearence as a girl, but grew coarse and overblown a few years later. For years she kept steady company with Albert Terry and married him at the age of thirty five. They had one daughter, Gwenyth. Reina died suddenly at the age of fifty and her mother spent the remainder of her eighty five years looking after Albert (whom she didn't like) and Gwenyth.
Auntie Polly 'took in sewing" from the time she left school, employing a couple of girls as a rule. Fred was rarely out of work, but they always seemed to be struggling. They bought '30 Loch Avenue, BALLARAT", a modest home, my father lending them the deposit for this. Years later when Fred's sixtieth birthday came around and they collected his insurance, Auntie offered Dad the 120 pounds still owing to him, but he wouldn't take it. That was a lot of money then.
He was very fond of Polly, who corresponded frequently with all her family. She was a good letter writer, which was more than her brother Bill was. He did reply to Carty when he wrote for parental permission for us to marry, but the only other person he ever wrote to was Mother, when she was away from home.
When Polly holidayed with us at the beach, she loved to swim - she could do a little frog-like breast stroke - and I remember her arriving late one Thursday night, and going down to a small rock pool in the dark, for a quick dip.Otherwise she would have had to wait until Saturday, because one daren't start swimming on a Friday. And , of course, to do so on Sunday was taboo also.
After Fred Smith's death, Polly often spent much of the winter in St Arnaud - Reina was alive then - because it was so cold in Ballarat. She was a welcome visitor, Mother being as fond of her as Dad. She was there with us when, in 1940, both my parents died. (Notes by Alice May Moore)

I remember Auntie Polly, probably because of the above-mentioned visits when I was living at "Alston" until 1939. She and Auntie Allie (Worsley) came to "Woodburn" to visit me when I was ill. I do not know what the illness was, but it was sufficiently severe and long lasting to require a trained nurse ( Sister Chalmers) to look after me.
I have a mental picture of them in the dining room where I was lying on the couch and Auntie Polly putting a stitch in the neck of the white knitted dog which she had given me ( as a baby,I think) to brace its head up. Auntie Allie had given me a pink knitted lamb and they both accompanied me to bed always, but I really preferred Doggy - and Auntie Polly, perhaps because she was a more familiar figure, perhaps because of her attractive personality. I was once taken to call on her in Ballarat, when or with whom I don't know, but we had to wait to see Gwenyth, of whom I remember nothing. (Ida Mary Dowty)
