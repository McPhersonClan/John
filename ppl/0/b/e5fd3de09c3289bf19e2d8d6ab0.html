Narrative

Judging by a photo that used to hang on our sitting room wall, Alf was in appearance very like his brothers Will 7 Jack (as someone was oft quoted as saying, " Jack & Bill. Aren't they alike? More especially Bill") Soon after he was married, he and Bill each decided to set up in business for himself and when we moved to Tungemah, Alf bought a place in St Arnaud. He & his wife Harriett had two small sons and a third was expected when Alf contracted pneumonia. He died in April, 1901. Jack took over the business and adopted Billy, the second boy. Harold, the eldest, went to live with his mother's people in Bealiba and Harriett kept the new baby, named after his father. Later, we saw a good deal of Harold when he attended our High School. He was a quiet, pleasant, likeable fellow. Handsome, too.
In Mildura in 1927 I was in a cafe when there entered a man bearing such a marked resemblance to Harold - and to Sandy - and young Jack - that I felt sure he was young Alf. Knowing that he had lost an eye in an accident in boyhood, I stopped at his table long enough to look him in the eye and then greeted him by name. He worked as a storeman at a weir and lock being built further along the Murray, and after that we often used to meet. His hobby was collecting snakes. He spent his weekly half holiday hunting them - thought nothing of carrying two or three of them by the tail - no danger at all, as long as one flicked them every now and then - wouldn't shoot them, except in the head, as he didn't want to spoil the skins. He supplemented his income by selling these, the skins, not the snakes. He didn't bother to carry a snake-bite outfit and had only been bitten once - when he lost his footing as he walked along a narrow embankment and, slithering awkwardly down down the slope, he forgot to flick.
