Narrative

Left England at the age of twenty five to make his fortune on the Victorian goldfields.
His mother worrried because he went in "one of those new-fangled steam ships" instead of a good reliable sailing ship and she wept when she heard of his marriage in 1867 at the age of thirty three because "Now Joe will never come back" - and he never didHe married Martha Edgell in Maldon and it is probable that all their family were born and brought up there. Hannah said that she left there when she was about two years old. I know very little about those years, except that the family was poor, and the boys were' wild little beggars', who more often roamed the bush than attended school. the mother of Henry Handel Richardson (born, like Dad, in 1870) kept the Post Office there when H.H.R. was about ten years old. And young William Moore was born in Maldon, presumably about the same year as Dad, so the three of them may well have been enrolled at the same school, back in 1880.
I don't know why the four Bales boys became bakers, or their sisters dressmakers, but I do know that all were very good at their trades.
Of my grandmother's family, I only know that my father had a poor opinion of them. Yet when he and Uncle Jack went to Melbourne to see the American Fleet in 1908, he told me he was going to stay with "my aunt, not your aunt." She must have been an Edgell.
I well remember my grandmother and was quite fond of her. She often stayed in St.Arnaud for long periods. She was shortish and plump. My father found her a trifle exasperating, my mother thought her irritating in the extreme - the feeling may well have been mutual, though Grandma was always aimiable. She had a tendency to bang a light-hearted tambourine in the local Salvation Army band, for one thing, and we were awfully respectable, church-going Presbyterians.
Though Grandma got on well enough with all her children, there was not that strong parental devotion that characterised the Hills.. She was casual about things and though she would work hard when the spirit moved her, she was quite capable of putting things in to soak and then forgetting them for a week. She did a lot of handwork- crochet and decorated needlework and had boxes of half-finished stuff. I sometimes have a tendency to do likewise, but the Hill in me won't let me get away with it.
She was pretty as a girl, and charming and gay and unreliable. She was only eighteen when they married, and they were poor and had eight children. After she was widowed, Polly(Mary Ann) always paid her rent, so she wouldn't be liable to put out on the footpath. Then when Polly was ill with a fever and earned nothing for six weeks she was chagrined to discover that her mother hadn't done anything about the rent. Polly paid up.
If my father was sometimes critical of his mother, he dearly loved his father. Just after Pat was born, Grandfather was killed when he was demolishing an old house, and his death was a bitter grief to them all. In Dad's absence, Mother christened the new baby, but Dad couldn't bear him to be called Joe, so he was "Bright" until he was about twelve, and thereafter answered to the name of Pat - to all but his parents..
