Bales, William Joseph

Birth Name Bales, William Joseph
Gender male
Age at Death 70 years, 5 days

Narrative

Born and brought up in Maldon where Henry Handel Richardson's mother ( born like William in 1870) kept the Post Office there when H.H.R. was about ten years old so they may have gone to school together.
Notes by A.M.Moore.
" We were all intensely proud of our father - of his integrity, his kindness, his gentle humour - of everything about him, and thought him the finest of his family. He hadn't the facile friendliness of his brothers. Courteous and kindly though he was, he was more selective in his friendships and had strong and enduring opinions.He quarrelled with nobody, but he didn't approve of everybody.
In appearance he was neat and smallish - about 5ft. 8ins in height (" Little men are the best bakers", five-year old Perce once said). he was handsome, with a strong profile, but, regrettably, had lost a lot of his curly brown hair. He had deft hands and small, trim feet and had been a good dancer and a good runner. As a boy, he had dodged school when he could and had roamed the bush, catching birds and chasing goannas. One day he went up a tree after one of these and when it reached the top, it turned and came back down the trunk and down his trunk, as he used to shudder to tell.

He was a particularly good tradesman and the business he conducted was most successful. It was late night shopping on a Saturday, but every alternate Wednesday was bakers' holiday. It was his custom to go shooting then - fishing in the district there was none! He became a keen gardener after he retired and as keen a bowler. He had been a generous boss.He retired in November 1927, and, feeling that Jim couldn't be expected to be Santa Claus to the staff at Christmas, he gave Barney & Alec each a cheque for 10 pounds, Brunton 5 pounds & the boy 2 pounds. And then, because Barney loved a bet and would take the cheque home to his wife, he slipped him 2 pounds in cash. In those days bakers were paid 7 pounds odd a week, more than day-time workers earned.

Always he was very fond of babies and claimed to know personally the occupant of every pram in St.Arnaud. His keen sense of humour was sedate rather than uproarious. He never forgot that he was the eldest brother and helped every member of his family at one time or another and did it cheerfully as a matter of course. He did no man an ill turn.

My parents lived happily together. If there were no endearments, there was a very close bond. Both worked long hours. Dad had very little leisure - once a week, most weeks, a few hours shooting, or a walk up the street. Mother had almost none and no social life while we were growing up, a large family, a big old house and a shop to attend to." In addition, most of the employees boarded with the family & were fed by Nana. (F.S. Bales, 2000)

As an infant living with them, I was particularly devoted to Pa, but my father' s family said that Nana was self-effacing, allowing him the credit with me for things which she had done or provided. I think he had more leisure to play with me than she did and I remember, as a toddler, nagging him, when gardening, to wheel me up & down in the drive at "Alston" in my (outgrown) pram which was kept in the garage. To him goes the credit of stopping Dorothy from persisting in feeding me bananas, which, it was apparent, even at that age, were intolerable to me. (I.M.S.D)

He liked to have a bet ( a taste inherited by his daughter May) & had a special account, into which he paid 10 pounds a week, for use when a really good thing appeared. Once, when he was down the street, he saw glamourous new car in a showroom ( Kershaw"s?; Hamilton's?) and went in to look at it. He asked the salesman whose it was and received the reply "That's Bill Bales' new car". He was so taken with it that he used his special account, bought it then & there and drove it home! This was the black Dodge. No-one was allowed to drive it, but Uncle Frank, eventually and Dorothy . In fact it lasted her until after the war and she sold it only after she & George were living in Windsor, probably about 1948/9. ( Story from Donne Bales, March 2001).
