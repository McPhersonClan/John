Bales, James Theodore

Narrative

Uncle Jim was the youngest of the boys, a baker like his brothers. When he & Fanny Miller were married, they bought a business in birchip, the one Jack afterwards had. they worked hard - he was a good tradesman - they prospered - and decided to try their luck in Melbourne. Dad lent them 500 pounds to buy a place there. Such was the relationship between them that all Dad had for this was Jim's verbal thanks. Of course, he repaid it when he could afford to, but there was never any question of interest on it.
When Dad retired in 1927 he offered his brother the business for somewhat less than its market value. by then Jim had become a bit casual and it used to fret Dad that he didn't do as well as we had done. The business deteriorated under slap-happy management. ( My McPherson family always said that Nana was a very good manager, so perhaps that had something to do with it [I.M.S.D.])
Uncle was a nice fellow - genial, easy-going, light-hearted. He'd do anyone a good turn & enjoy doing it. In the years before he died he gradually lost the use of his legs, but when anyone enquired how he was he'd reply cheerfully "I'm fine. How are you?"
He & Aunt Fanny reared four children - Dorrie, married & living in St.Arnaud; Laurie & Les ( a baker); and Norma, whose husband is one of the Bray twins whom I taught in Birchip. Three of these are fair , but Laurie was not. I took him home for the school holidays in 1922 and well remember him trailing along behind Frank, who was behind Dad when they went shooting. He was a nice lad and, grown up, loved fishing & shooting too. Camped once on the Edwards River on a fishing trip he was so suntanned that a publican refused to serve him a drink - under the Blackfellows' Act. He was a POW in Japanese hands during the war and, although he survived to return and to marry, he died a few years later." (A.M. Moore)
Because of his baking experience, he was in the kitchen of the prison camp and Duncan McRae of Gre Gre North, who was also a prisoner in that camp, ascribed his survival to Laurie's assistance with food. Duncan looked like a skeleton when he returned and that was after a few months recuperation!
Laurie was running "The Cabin" tea rooms, an adjunct to the bakery, in St Arnaud in 1949 when I was at High School and, as a family favour, would let me have a half milkshake for sixpence on the one day of the week when I bought my lunch; one shilling then would buy me sixpence worth of chips & the half milkshake! (I.M.S.D.)
